KATHERINE JOHNSON
"Girls are capable of doing everything men are capable of doing. Sometimes they have more imagination than men.”

Katherine Johnson was a math prodigy at her school and advanced up the ranks in school. She joined the National Advisory
Committee for Aeronautics’ (later NASA) Langley laboratory in 1953. During 1956, her husband died of cancer. A
year later, Sputnik’s launch changed her life and began to work on Freedom 7, U.S.’ first manned spaceflight, by
solving trajectories. Her report was the first time a woman in the Flight Research Division had received credit as
an author in 1960. In 1962, NASA’s Friendship 7 mission gave Johnson the opportunity to play a larger role as Glenn
requested Johnson to check the trajectory number. The mission was a success. Johnson partook many other missions.
